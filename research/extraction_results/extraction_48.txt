UGANDA'S COMEBACK

Now, nearly a decade later, Uganda
boasts an economic growth rate of 7 per
cent, one of the highest on the continent.
Blessed with good rainfall and fertile soil,
the country can largely feed its people and
export food. Peace in the countryside has
spurred productivity and enabled rural
markets to thrive. The government is
promoting agricultural diversification and
modernization, efforts that are beginning
to bear fruit despite limited resources and
campetition for foreign markets. And a
rural population schooled in fear and
repression is finding its voice with a flour-
ishing of grass-roots organizations and a
women’s movement.

The Museveni government is draw-
ing accolades from the international
donors who will provide Uganda with
$820 million in aid during 1995. “Uganda
strongman a favorite of world leaders,”
says a New York Times headline, while the
Washington Post, another major opinion-
maker in the developed world, trumpets,
“Uganda, in comeback, attracts investors;
aid donors ‘see country’ really trying to
rebuild’ after dictators.”

The magnets for donors and in-
vestors are safety, economic liberalization,
minimal red tape and generous tax breaks.
