From NEK Video Links to Nationwide Production (1991-1998)`
------> Summary: Nigeria's home video film industry emerged 
in 1991 when NEK Video Links, led by Kenneth Nnebue, 
pioneered direct-to-home marketing of films shot in U-Matic 
format and sold on VHS cassettes. 
The first release, *Aje Ni Iyami* (March 1991), received 
enthusiastic response, with eight additional films produced 
by November 1991. Initially dominated by Yoruba-language productions, 
the industry rapidly expanded across ethnic groups, 
with producers releasing films in Igbo, Hausa, and English.

By the mid-1990s, economic downturn accelerated the shift from 
expensive celluloid (costing 6-8 million) to affordable video production, 
with an average of six films released monthly by 1997. 
However, rapid commercialization brought criticism: producers 
prioritized profit over quality, completing films in under a year 
when quality required four years. Industry figures like Ego Boyo and 
Dr. Ola Makinde complained about rushed productions, poor compensation for actors, 
and films celebrating "cultic, fetish" practices that damaged Nigeria's international image.
Despite quality concerns, pioneers like Jide Kosoko credited video production with 
keeping the industry "intact" during economic crisis. By 1996, the Nigerian Motion 
Pictures Association formed to organize guilds of technicians, producers, and actors, 
though the National Film and Video Censors Board struggled to regulate the flood of 
new releases.

Ego Boyo calling for improved production standards to 
compete with foreign films. However, by 1997, 
industry observers expressed alarm at a troubling trend: 
the proliferation of films glorifying witchcraft, cultism,

promoting indigenous culture and positive values. Critics like 
Ifeanyi Dike of the Nigerian Actors Guild warned that these productions, 
driven by commercial considerations rather than artistic merit, 
portrayed Nigeria negatively internationally and lacked the moral 
heroes of traditional storytelling, revealing a young industry grappling 
with the tension between profitability and cultural responsibility.

while honoring the legacy of theatre doyen Pa Hubert Ogunde. 
Nkem Owoh emerged as the highest-paid actor, signaling Nollywood's 
commercial viability even as it grappled with professionalizing its 
practices and protecting its economic interests.

The period also saw the emergence of distinct star personas and industry hierarchies, with publications tracking highest-paid actors, popular actresses, and individual career trajectories, reflecting Nollywood's evolution into a celebrity-driven entertainment economy with its own gossip culture, professional ethics debates, and discussions about artistic mission versus commercial pressures.

------> Summary: This period marked Nollywood's pursuit of international 
legitimacy and technical improvement. In 2008, 'Mirror of the Soul' 
became the first Nigerian film in 730 years accepted at Cannes Film Festival, 
shot on 35mm celluloid, symbolizing the industry's technical aspirations. 
Initiatives like Project Nollywood (backed by Ecobank) 
established 20 distribution channels targeting 1.3 million copies per film, 
while directors sought Hollywood technical support for cameras and special 
effects to 'compete favourably in the global market.' 
The Directors Guild of Nigeria president declared Nollywood had '
come of age' with improved quality, content, and equipment.

However, persistent challenges emerged. Government and industry leaders 
criticized poor technical quality, sexual harassment, and financial impropriety, 
with NTA Enterprises' Executive Director calling the industry 'a haven for crooks' 
who misappropriated investor funds. Internal debates arose over the 'Nollywood' 
terminology excluding Yoruba and Hausa films, while regulators banned actors Aki 
and Pawpaw from adult roles due to parental concerns. 
Despite criticism, the industry expanded with celebrity reality shows, 
foreign actor work permit proposals, and new awards like TIMAO recognizing 
indigenous African language films, demonstrating Nollywood's maturation 
amid growing pains.

------> Summary: This period marked a critical juncture where Nollywood's rapid commercial expansion collided with deepening concerns about professional standards and industry sustainability. The era was characterized by intense internal debates about quality versus quantity, with filmmakers like Kunle Afolayan criticizing the industry's global ranking as third only in volume, not quality, while veterans like Teco Benson defended Nigerian storytelling's inherent value, citing Hollywood's remake of his film 'Blood Diamond' as validation. The industry experienced significant fragmentation, with Yoruba filmmakers explicitly rejecting the 'Nollywood' label as exclusive to English-language productions, asserting their separate identity under ANTP. Simultaneously, the industry faced severe infrastructure and welfare challenges: established actors like Sam Dede withdrew for three years due to declining standards, veteran Prince James Uche was hospitalized without adequate support, and prominent actors engaged in public disputes over unpaid debts. Yet the period also showed international aspirations, with productions increasingly shot in England, collaborations with Hollywood for technical expertise, politically-conscious films like '30 Days' addressing corruption, and actresses like Stephanie Okereke gaining international recognition. The emergence of 'godfather' figures like Tunde Kelani, Zeb Ejiro, and Jide Kosoko suggested attempts to institutionalize mentorship and professional standards amid this chaotic maturation phase.
